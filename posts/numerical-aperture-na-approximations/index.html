Numerical Aperture (NA) approximations

Daniel Nicolai

2020-06-29 16:50

Comments

A not so short inquiry into NA approximations and their validity.

The definition for NA is given by the formula \[\mathrm{NA} = \sin{\theta}\] where \(\theta\) is half the opening angle of the light cone accepted by the imaging system.

For small angles the paraxial approximations \[\sin{\theta} \approx \theta,\: \cos{\theta} \approx 1\: \text{and}\: \tan{\theta} = \frac{\sin{\theta}}{\cos{\theta}} \approx \theta\] can be used for calculating \(\theta\), \(\sin{\theta}\) and \(\tan{\theta}\).

For a thin lens \(\theta = \arctan{\frac{D}{2f}}\), where \(D\) and \(f\) are respectively the diameter and the focal length of the lens. To obtain \(\frac{D}{2f}\) we use \(\frac{D}{2f} = \tan{\theta}\)

Table 1 and 2 show the angles below which the relative error for the paraxial approximation are less then 10% and 5% respectively.

Table 1: θ where error < 10%
  rad deg
arctan(θ) vs θ 0.569 32.60
sin(θ) vs θ 0.748 42.86
NA(θ) vs θ 0.458 26.24
NA(θ) vs tan(θ) 0.341 19.54
Table 2: θ where error < 5%
  rad deg tan θ
NA(θ) vs tan(θ) 0.243 13.92 0.24

Table 1 shows that using the approximation \(\mathrm{NA}(\theta) \approx \frac{D}{2f}\) gives more than 10% for angles larger than 0.34 rad (tan θ = 0.35) or 19 degrees, while the errors become larger than 5% already for angles of 0.24 rad or 13 degrees.
